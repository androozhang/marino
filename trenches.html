Giant Tubeworms

Giant tubeworms live on the floor of the ocean, usually near black smokers, a type of hydrothermal vent. They can tolerate extreme heat up to 54Â°C and could withstand up to 2,000 pounds of pressure per square inch. Giant tube worms live in a mutually beneficial relationship with microorganisms like certain bacterias. In return, bacteria give food to giant tube worms for the accommodation and chemical compounds that giant tube worms provide. They are soft and generally protect animals from other predators. Their lifespan is unknown but will die out once the hydrothermal vents are gone.

Amphipods

The name Amphipoda means "different-footed", unlike other species, amphipods have different appendage types. Out of the 7,000 discovered species, 5,000 of them are classified into one suborder, the Gammaridea. Their sizes range from .1 to 34 centimeters. Most amphipods are detritivores or scavengers that eat small insects in the ocean and crustaceans. They use the front two pairs of legs forming large claws to catch their prey. Matured females have a marsupium, or brood pouch, which they use to hold their eggs in while they are fertilized and ready to be hatched.

Cusk Eel

The name cusk eel may be misleading since they are not true eels. They have many features in common with eels but there is one feature that can distinguish them apart, which is their fins. The pelvic fins under the cusk eels enable fish to move forward, turn, stop, and move up and down. They spend most of their time near the seafloor burrowing themselves into the mud to hide from predators and sneak up on prey.
